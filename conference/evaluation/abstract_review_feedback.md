## Primary Reviewer 1 — Dr. Amara Osei-Bonsu

**Lens: Methodological rigor, study design, and epidemiological soundness**

### Strengths

- **Clear, testable research question.** The central question — whether a decentralized, partner-led model can match the longitudinal data integrity of a directly managed site — is well-defined and operationally measurable. This is a strength many abstracts lack.
- **Concrete metrics.** The authors specify measurable benchmarks (12 samples/week target, turnaround time in days) and report against them, which makes the comparison between sites transparent and reproducible.
- **Head-to-head comparison design.** Comparing a domestic site against an international partner-led site using the same platform (Cepheid GeneXpert) and the same target pathogens is a smart design choice that controls for many potential confounders.
- **Surprising and honest result.** The finding that the Zambian site *outperformed* the Madison site on both frequency and turnaround time is noteworthy and, to the authors' credit, reported straightforwardly rather than buried.

### Concerns

- **Operational metrics are reported, but where are the epidemiological results?** The abstract tells us *how often* samples were tested and *how quickly*, but says almost nothing about pathogen detection rates, positivity trends, or seasonal patterns. For a surveillance study, this is a significant omission. Were pathogens actually detected? At what rates? Did the two sites show comparable or divergent circulation patterns? Without this, the abstract evaluates a *workflow*, not a *surveillance system*.
- **The statistical claim needs more context.** A p-value of <0.001 is reported for the turnaround time difference (2.0 vs. 3.0 days), but what test was used? Given the likely non-normal distribution of turnaround times, was a non-parametric test applied? What were the interquartile ranges or standard deviations? A median difference of one day is operationally meaningful, but the abstract should briefly characterize the variability.
- **What explains Madison's underperformance?** The domestic site hit its weekly target only 46.9% of the time compared to Zambia's 87.5%. This is a striking result that is left entirely unexplained. Was it staffing? Competing priorities? Equipment downtime? Understanding *why* matters both for interpreting the comparison and for the broader argument about decentralized models.
- **Sample quality and validity are unaddressed.** Air sampling introduces well-known challenges around sample degradation, environmental interference, and machine calibration. The abstract does not mention any quality control measures, positive/negative controls, or concordance checks. Turnaround time and frequency alone do not establish "diagnostic rigor."
- **The timeframe is short.** Eight months of data (July 2025–March 2026) captures one respiratory season. The claim of "longitudinal data integrity" would be stronger with acknowledgment that this is a preliminary assessment of a system that needs multi-season validation.

### Recommendation

The operational comparison is interesting and the results are genuinely surprising, but the abstract over-promises relative to what it delivers. It claims to validate a surveillance model but only presents workflow logistics. To strengthen this for future submissions, report at least summary pathogen findings, address the quality assurance question, and be more transparent about analytical methods.

**Score: Poster presentation.** The comparison design and counterintuitive results would generate good discussion at a poster session, but the evidentiary gaps make it premature for an oral talk.

## Primary Reviewer 2 — Dr. Ricardo Méndez-Ferreira

**Lens: Community partnership, equity, and ethical framing**

### Strengths

- **The framing is ambitious and welcome.** "Scientific sovereignty" is a term with real weight in global health discourse, and it is encouraging to see UW-Madison researchers engaging with it directly rather than using softer euphemisms. The explicit contrast between "extractive research" and "sustainable alliances" signals awareness of the field's problematic history.
- **The study design embodies its values.** Rather than simply arguing for partner-led models, the authors built one and measured its performance. The fact that the partner-led site outperformed the directly managed site is a powerful proof-of-concept that goes beyond rhetoric.
- **"Horizontal partnerships" language is appropriate.** The abstract avoids the condescending "capacity building" framing that implies a one-way transfer of knowledge, at least in its concluding section. The notion that the Zambian site operates as a "technically independent partner" is a meaningful claim.

### Concerns

- **The Zambian partners are invisible.** This is my most significant concern. For an abstract about scientific sovereignty, the actual Zambian partners are remarkably absent. Who are they? Which institution(s)? Were they involved in study design, or did they implement a protocol handed to them? Is there Zambian co-authorship? The abstract reads as if UW-Madison designed the model, deployed it, and is now evaluating its partners' performance — which is closer to a managed subcontract than to sovereignty.
- **"Sovereignty" requires more than operational competence.** The abstract defines success as the Zambian site matching the Madison site's data integrity. But sovereignty implies ownership of the research questions, the data, and the decision-making. Who decides what pathogens to test? Who owns the data? Who publishes? Who sets the research agenda? The abstract measures compliance with a UW-designed protocol, which is a necessary but insufficient condition for the sovereignty it claims.
- **"Capacity building" contradiction.** The practical application section says this work "moves beyond data sharing toward true capacity building," but this language still centers UW-Madison as the entity building the capacity of others. If the Zambian site is truly sovereign, the framing should center their agency — what they chose to do, what they gained on their own terms.
- **Community context is missing.** What is the healthcare context in Southern Province, Zambia? What are the existing public health priorities there? Does respiratory pathogen surveillance align with local needs and priorities, or was this agenda set externally? Without this context, the reader cannot assess whether the partnership is genuinely responsive to local priorities.
- **Ethical review is not mentioned.** Was this study reviewed by an IRB or ethics board — both at UW-Madison and in Zambia? For a study explicitly about power dynamics in global health research, this omission is conspicuous.

### Recommendation

I appreciate the aspiration, but the abstract has a gap between its framing (scientific sovereignty, horizontal partnership) and its evidence (one site outperformed another on operational metrics designed by UW-Madison). For future submissions, the authors should center the partner perspective, describe the governance structure of the partnership, and honestly assess which elements of sovereignty have been achieved and which remain aspirational.

**Score: Poster presentation.** The topic is highly relevant and the framing is directionally right, but the absence of partner voice and the shallow treatment of sovereignty prevent this from being ready for an oral talk.

## Primary Reviewer 3 — Dr. Priya Chakraborty

**Lens: Health systems impact, policy relevance, scalability, and the "so what?" question**

### Strengths

- **Direct policy application is identified.** The connection to masking policies and healthcare setting recommendations is concrete and actionable. This is not purely academic work — it has a clear end-user.
- **The model is inherently about sustainability.** Unlike many global health projects that depend on continuous external support, this one explicitly aims to create a node that can function independently. If the operational data hold up, this has real implications for how surveillance networks are structured.
- **Relevant to current global health architecture debates.** Post-COVID, there is significant international attention on building distributed surveillance capacity. This work speaks directly to that conversation.

### Concerns

- **The "so what?" is underdeveloped.** The abstract claims the work "informs public health recommendations and healthcare setting policies like masking," but provides no evidence that it has actually done so. Have the surveillance data been shared with local public health authorities in either site? Have any policy decisions been influenced? Claiming policy relevance without demonstrating it weakens the argument.
- **Scalability is not addressed.** The model works at two sites. What would it take to scale to ten? Fifty? What are the cost implications, the training requirements, the supply chain dependencies (Cepheid cartridges are expensive and proprietary)? A systems-strengthening argument needs to engage with these questions.
- **Cepheid platform dependency is a vulnerability.** The entire model rests on proprietary GeneXpert technology. What happens when cartridges are unavailable, prices increase, or the technology is superseded? True systems strengthening would at least acknowledge this dependency and discuss alternatives or contingency planning.
- **No cost data.** For a feasibility study, the absence of any cost information is a gap. Decision-makers who might replicate this model need to know what it costs to establish and sustain a node.
- **The comparison may not generalize.** The Madison site's underperformance could reflect unique local factors (staffing models, competing lab priorities) rather than anything intrinsic about centralized vs. decentralized models. Without understanding *why* the sites performed differently, it is difficult to draw systems-level conclusions.

### Recommendation

This work has genuine policy potential, but the abstract stays at the proof-of-concept level without connecting to the systems and policy questions that would make it compelling to a broader audience. Future submissions should include evidence of actual policy uptake, cost analysis, and a candid discussion of scalability constraints.

**Score: Poster presentation.** Good concept with real-world relevance, but the policy and systems arguments need development before this is ready for an oral talk.

## Consensus Recommendation

**Decision: Poster Presentation**

The committee recommends this abstract for a **poster presentation with a scheduled question-and-answer session**, rather than a poster-only display or an oral talk.

### Rationale

The abstract presents a timely, well-framed question about decentralized surveillance models and produces a genuinely interesting result — the partner-led site outperforming the directly managed site. The topic is highly relevant to the symposium's mission of showcasing UW-Madison's global health activity, and the partnership model itself is commendable.

However, the abstract is not ready for an oral talk for several reasons: the gap between the "scientific sovereignty" framing and the evidence presented (which measures operational compliance, not sovereignty); the absence of actual epidemiological findings; the lack of partner voice or governance detail; and the underdeveloped policy and systems arguments. These are not fatal flaws — they are areas for growth that a poster discussion could help develop.

### Summary of Key Feedback for Improvement

1. **Include pathogen detection results.** Even summary data (positivity rates, seasonal trends, cross-site comparisons) would substantially strengthen the surveillance argument.

2. **Name and center your partners.** Identify the Zambian institution(s) and describe the governance structure. If there is co-authorship, make it visible. If not, address why.

3. **Interrogate your own framing.** "Scientific sovereignty" is a strong claim. Be explicit about what has been achieved (operational independence) and what remains aspirational (data ownership, agenda-setting). Honest self-assessment is more persuasive than overclaiming.

4. **Explain the Madison underperformance.** This surprising finding is one of the most interesting parts of the abstract, but it is left unexplored. Understanding it would strengthen the comparative analysis.

5. **Add methodological detail.** Describe the statistical test used, report variability measures for turnaround time, and mention any quality control procedures.

6. **Address scalability and cost.** A feasibility study should help readers assess whether the model can be replicated. Cost per node, training time, and supply chain considerations would be valuable additions.

7. **Demonstrate policy uptake.** If surveillance data have informed any local decisions (masking policies, public health alerts), describe this concretely. If not yet, frame it as a future direction rather than a current outcome.

8. **Consider your audience breadth.** The Global Health Symposium spans many disciplines. Brief contextual definitions for technical terms (GeneXpert, air samplers) would make the work accessible to the full audience.
