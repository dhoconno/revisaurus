## Part I: Review Committee Feedback

### Dr. Amara Osei-Bonsu — Epidemiology

**Overall impression:** The poster has improved on the abstract in one important way — the Discussion section now explains Madison's underperformance (healthcare staffing and competing priorities causing collection lag), which was a gap I flagged in the abstract review. However, my core concern remains: this poster presents a surveillance study with no surveillance results.

**Specific feedback:**

- **The pathogen data gap persists.** The poster title says "Respiratory Pathogen Surveillance," and the methods describe testing for Influenza A/B, RSV, and SARS-CoV-2 using GeneXpert. But Figures 4 and 5 only show turnaround time and testing frequency — operational logistics. Were any pathogens detected? At what rates? Were there seasonal trends? Cross-site differences in positivity? This is the most important data a surveillance poster should present, and it is entirely absent. Even a single figure showing positivity rates by pathogen and site would transform this poster.
- **The statistical test is now visible but still problematic.** Figure 4's caption specifies a two-sample t-test for the turnaround time comparison. However, turnaround time data are almost certainly right-skewed (you cannot have a TaT below zero, but outliers can extend far to the right). The boxplots in Figure 4 appear to confirm this — the Madison data has substantial upper-tail spread. A Wilcoxon rank-sum test would be more appropriate. Additionally, the IQR is shown but not reported numerically in the caption. Add it.
- **"Insect ingress" is mentioned but unexplained.** The Discussion references infrastructure hurdles including insect ingress as a challenge the Choma site overcame. This is actually a fascinating operational detail that deserves one more sentence — did insects contaminate samples? Damage equipment? How was it resolved? This kind of practical knowledge is exactly what makes poster sessions valuable.
- **Sample size is never stated.** How many total cartridges were tested at each site? The weekly counts in Figure 5 allow back-calculation, but this should be stated explicitly.

### Dr. Ricardo Méndez-Ferreira — Community-Engaged Research & Health Equity

**Overall impression:** I am genuinely pleased to see two things the abstract lacked: Zambian co-authors (Passwell Munachoonga and Mutinta Hamahuwa from Macha Research Trust) and the phrase "co-created" appearing twice. These are meaningful improvements. However, I still have concerns about the depth of partnership representation.

**Specific feedback:**

- **Macha Research Trust is named but not described.** Poster viewers will not all know what MRT is. One sentence establishing MRT as an independent Zambian research institution with its own history and capacity would go a long way toward conveying that this is a genuine partnership, not a subcontract.
- **The Theory of Change (Figure 2) is revealing — and not entirely in the way intended.** The logic model flows left to right from UW-Madison inputs to Choma outcomes. This visual structure implies a unidirectional transfer — UW provides inputs, Zambia receives. If the partnership is truly horizontal, the ToC should reflect bidirectional value. What does UW-Madison learn from Macha Research Trust? What expertise do the Zambian partners bring that UW lacks?
- **"Co-created" needs evidence.** The word appears but the poster does not describe what co-creation looked like in practice. Were protocols jointly designed? Did Zambian partners shape the research question? Were there disagreements, and how were they resolved? At a poster session, presenters should be prepared for these questions — and the poster itself should provide at least a hint of the answer.
- **The Causal Loop Diagram (Figure 1) is a strength.** It effectively communicates the reinforcing cycle between local data production, public trust, infrastructure investment, and reduced dependence on extractive models. This is one of the most thoughtful visual elements on the poster.
- **Consider Zambian co-author positioning.** In global health, author order signals contribution and power. Having both Zambian co-authors in middle positions is common but worth reflecting on. If Munachoonga or Hamahuwa led site operations, their contribution may warrant more prominent authorship.

### Dr. Priya Chakraborty — Policy & Health Systems

**Overall impression:** The Theory of Change model is a welcome addition that was absent from the abstract. It maps a plausible pathway from inputs to impacts. But the poster still does not demonstrate that any of those downstream outcomes (community health, economic development, public policy, scientific sovereignty) have actually materialized.

**Specific feedback:**

- **Show evidence of policy uptake or at minimum, connection.** The abstract claimed this work "informs public health recommendations and healthcare setting policies like masking." The poster's conclusion says this is "a blueprint for non-extractive global health infrastructure." These are strong claims. Has any public health authority in Choma or Southern Province used these data? Has the Wisconsin data influenced any local policy? If so, say it. If not, reframe as future direction.
- **The ToC's later-stage outcomes are aspirational, and that's fine — if labeled as such.** The right side of Figure 2 lists Community Health, Economic Development, Public Policy and Trust, and Scientific Sovereignty as outcomes. These are appropriate long-term goals, but the 8-month study does not provide evidence for any of them. The figure caption partially acknowledges this ("represent broader effects"), but the poster should be more explicit that these are hypothesized downstream impacts, not demonstrated results.
- **Cost and scalability remain unaddressed.** Cepheid GeneXpert cartridges cost approximately $15–20 USD each. At 12 cartridges/week, that is roughly $10,000/year in consumables alone per site, plus equipment, shipping, and personnel. For a model that claims to be a "blueprint" for global health infrastructure, economic feasibility is a critical missing piece. Even a single sentence acknowledging the cost structure would help.
- **What happens when UW-Madison steps back?** The poster argues for scientific sovereignty and independent operation. But who currently funds the cartridges? The equipment? If funding stops, does the node survive? Sustainability should be addressed honestly.

## Part II: Poster Session Attendee Perspectives

### Dr. Thandiwe Mwanza — Zambian Public Health Physician

*Senior Medical Officer, Southern Provincial Health Office, Zambia. Attending the symposium as a visiting scholar. She has direct experience with health systems in Southern Province and cares deeply about whether research partnerships deliver tangible benefits to her communities.*

**Feedback:**

- **This work matters to us, and I am glad to see it represented here.** Environmental pathogen surveillance in healthcare settings is not something most district health offices in Zambia can do independently. If this model works, it could meaningfully inform our seasonal preparedness planning.
- **But I want to know: who sees the results?** Does the Choma District Health Office receive reports from this surveillance? Are results shared with facility infection prevention committees? The poster describes a data pipeline but does not describe a reporting or dissemination pipeline. Data that stays in a research database does not strengthen public health systems.
- **The phrase "lower-resource settings" in the Figure 2 caption is imprecise.** Southern Province is not uniformly "lower-resource." Choma is a provincial capital with a referral hospital. Characterizing the setting more specifically would be more respectful and more accurate.
- **I notice the air samplers are different between sites.** Figure 3 shows InBio Apollo in Zambia and ThermoFisher AerosolSense in Madison. This is a potential confounder that the poster does not address. Are these platforms comparable in collection efficiency? If not, how does this affect the cross-site comparison?

### Prof. Michael Torres — Agricultural & Environmental Health

*Professor, UW-Madison Department of Population Health Sciences, with expertise in One Health approaches and environmental monitoring. He is interested in how surveillance methods could apply beyond clinical settings.*

**Feedback:**

- **The air sampling approach is interesting and I would like to know more about it.** What particle sizes are captured? What is the sampling flow rate and duration? How does environmental temperature and humidity affect collection efficiency? These are details that matter for anyone who might want to adapt this methodology.
- **Have the authors considered non-clinical sampling sites?** The poster focuses on healthcare settings, but respiratory pathogen surveillance in markets, schools, or transport hubs could provide community-level circulation data. This would be a natural extension — and more aligned with the "public health" framing than hospital-only sampling.
- **The connection to One Health is implicit but not developed.** Respiratory pathogen surveillance at the human-animal-environment interface is a major priority. If any of the detected pathogens have zoonotic relevance (Influenza A certainly does), this could be positioned within the One Health framework to reach a broader audience.

### Maya Chen, MPH — Student Reviewer (from original committee)

**Feedback on accessibility and communication:**

- **The poster is visually busy.** The five-color section header scheme (teal, red, gold, green, magenta) creates visual competition rather than hierarchy. A more restrained palette with one or two accent colors would help the eye move through the content in the intended order.
- **Reading path is unclear.** Scientific posters are typically read in columns (left → right, top → bottom within each column), but this poster mixes full-width elements (title, methods) with side-by-side elements (discussion/conclusion) in a way that makes the flow ambiguous. At a busy poster session, viewers will spend 30–60 seconds deciding whether to engage. The visual path needs to be instantly legible.
- **Figure 2 (Theory of Change) is too dense for poster scale.** The text within the ToC boxes will be very small when printed at standard poster size. Consider simplifying to show only the key nodes, or enlarging the figure and reducing surrounding text.
- **Typo in the title: "Scientifc" should be "Scientific."** This appears in both the poster title and the Figure 1 caption. Fix before printing.
- **The term "insect ingress" in the Discussion is jargon that will confuse non-technical viewers.** Reword to something like "challenges such as insects entering sampling equipment."

## Part III: Design Review

### Dr. Sarah Lindström — Science Communication & Information Design

*Associate Professor, UW-Madison Department of Life Sciences Communication, with a specialization in visual communication of scientific data. She consults on poster design for graduate students and has published on the cognitive science of scientific figure interpretation.*

**Design Assessment:**

**1. Visual hierarchy and reading flow**

The poster lacks a clear visual hierarchy. All five section headers compete for attention equally due to the high-saturation color blocks (teal, red/orange, gold, green, magenta). In information design, color should encode meaning or priority, not merely decoration. The viewer's eye has no entry point and no guided path. Recommendation: establish a single accent color for primary sections, use value (light/dark) variation for secondary structure, and ensure the reading order follows a predictable grid — either a clear three-column or Z-pattern layout.

**2. Color palette**

The current palette uses five saturated, unrelated hues for section headers. This creates a "rainbow effect" that fragments the poster visually and implies categorical distinctions between sections that do not actually exist (Introduction and Methods are not categorically different types of content). Recommendation: adopt a two-color scheme — a primary color for section headers and structural elements, and a single contrasting accent for emphasis (e.g., key findings, statistical significance). Use neutral tones (grays, off-whites) for backgrounds and text containers.

**3. Figure design and integration**

- Figure 1 (Causal Loop Diagram) is conceptually strong but visually inconsistent with the rest of the poster. It uses its own color scheme (greens, blues, reds) that does not match the section header palette, creating a third visual system.
- Figure 3 (Protocol diagram) effectively communicates the sampling workflow and is the most visually successful element on the poster. The side-by-side comparison with shared process steps clearly shows what is common and what differs between sites.
- Figures 4 and 5 are functional but could be improved. The boxplot (Fig. 4) should include the sample sizes (n=) on the axis. The weekly testing time series (Fig. 5) would benefit from annotation — mark the weeks where equipment or staffing issues caused dips.

**4. Typography**

The poster uses what appears to be a default BioRender template font. Text sizes appear appropriate for the section headers but the body text and figure captions will be difficult to read at standard poster viewing distance (3–6 feet). Recommendation: body text should be a minimum of 24pt for a standard poster; figure captions should be no smaller than 20pt. Use a maximum of two typefaces — one sans-serif for headers and labels, one (optionally different) for body text.

**5. White space and density**

The poster is text-heavy for a poster format. The Introduction section in particular contains a full paragraph of background text that duplicates information the viewer can glean from the title and figures. Posters are not papers — they are visual arguments. Recommendation: reduce body text by 40–50%, rely on figures to carry the narrative, and use the freed space to increase figure sizes and add breathing room between elements.

**6. Key finding visibility**

The single most important finding — the Zambian partner-led site outperformed the directly managed Madison site — is not visually distinguished from any other text on the poster. This should be the element a viewer sees within the first 5 seconds of approaching the poster. Recommendation: create a prominent callout box or banner with this finding, positioned in the upper portion of the results section, in the accent color.

## Summary of Prioritized Improvements

### Must-fix before the symposium (3 days)

1. **Fix the typo.** "Scientifc" → "Scientific" in the title and Figure 1 caption.
2. **Add a key finding callout.** Create a visually prominent summary of the main result (Zambian site outperformed Madison on both metrics). This should be visible from 6 feet away.
3. **Simplify the color palette.** Reduce to two colors plus neutrals. This single change will dramatically improve visual coherence.
4. **Reduce text density.** Cut the Introduction body text by half. The background information can be communicated verbally during the presentation.
5. **State sample sizes.** Add total cartridges tested per site, either in the results text or on the figures.
6. **Report IQR numerically.** Add interquartile ranges to the Figure 4 caption.
7. **Clarify the air sampler difference.** Add one sentence acknowledging that different samplers were used and how this was accounted for (or note it as a limitation).

### Should-improve if time allows

8. **Add even summary pathogen detection results.** Even a simple table of positivity rates by pathogen and site would address the central gap identified by multiple reviewers.
9. **Add one sentence describing Macha Research Trust** as an independent Zambian research institution.
10. **Simplify Figure 2 (Theory of Change)** by enlarging key nodes and reducing text within boxes, or consider replacing with a simplified version.
11. **Reframe aspirational outcomes honestly** — label downstream ToC impacts as hypothesized rather than demonstrated.
12. **Prepare verbal responses** for questions about cost, sustainability, data governance, and pathogen results even if they cannot all be added to the poster.

### For future presentations

13. Add pathogen detection data as a primary results figure.
14. Include cost-per-site or cost-per-sample data.
15. Describe the data governance and reporting structure (who sees the data in Zambia).
16. Consider Zambian co-author positioning and whether it reflects actual contribution.
17. Address the One Health angle if Influenza A data support it.
