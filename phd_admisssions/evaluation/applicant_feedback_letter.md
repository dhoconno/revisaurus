Dear Timothy,

Thank you for your application to the Cellular and Molecular Biology PhD Program at the University of Wisconsin–Madison. Before we share our feedback, we want to be transparent with you about what we reviewed: this committee evaluated your Personal Statement only. We did not have access to your Research Statement, transcripts, CV, three letters of recommendation, or the 400-word supplemental UW-Madison/CMB statement. **The committee has recommended that you be invited for an interview.**

## Your scores

The CMB rubric evaluates four categories on a 1.0–4.0 scale in 0.5 increments (4.0 Outstanding, 3.0 Excellent, 2.0 Good, 1.0 Poor). The first two categories carry more weight in the overall score; the last two cap at 3.0 Excellent.

- Engagement in Research (Most Important):            **3.0**
- Resilience / Grit / Work Ethic (Most Important):    **3.0**
- Readiness / Fit for Program (caps at 3.0):          **2.5**
- Why UW-Madison & CMB? (caps at 3.0):                **2.5**
- **Overall (weighted, not averaged): 3.0 (Excellent)**

Below, we explain how each score was reached and, where reviewers saw different things in your materials, how the committee weighed those perspectives.

## Engagement in Research — 3.0

We read a career trajectory that contains real science done at real scale under real pressure. Your account of moving from high-throughput diagnostic shift work into the AMD core laboratory, then into sequencing-based surveillance for SARS-CoV-2 variants, hepatitis A virus, norovirus, and dengue, and eventually into methodology evaluation for agnostic and metagenomic platforms, describes a genuinely interdisciplinary scientific life. The Oropouche orthobunyavirus passage — where a methodological choice produced a full genome, linked a Florida case to an outbreak in Brazil, and then fed back into primer design for a diagnostic RT-PCR — was the single strongest moment in your statement for us. It shows the loop between method and question that we associate with research thinking, not just laboratory operations.

Our reviewers converged on 3.0 Excellent. Prof. Alder, whose lens is sharpest on scientific rigor and on the 4.0 vs 3.0 distinction, initially scored lower (2.5). His concern was that across the statement, your work is framed more often as *what was done* than as *what question was asked, what hypothesis was tested, and how ambiguous results were interpreted*. Prof. Bemis read the same material with attention to trajectory — how you have grown between early and recent experiences — and found substantive intellectual engagement, particularly in the Grubaugh collaboration and the Oropouche work. Prof. Chen mapped your described work onto CMB's virology, molecular and genome biology of microbes, and systems biology focus groups and found genuine interdisciplinary engagement. G. Delgado noted curiosity and a professional-scientific hunger in your writing that felt authentic rather than performative.

The CMB rubric reserves the Outstanding (4.0) rating for applicants whose research engagement is clearly self-driven and independently motivated — not just responsive to a mentor or an institution. We could see evidence of initiative in your statement (seeking out Dr. Grubaugh, advocating for agnostic sequencing evaluation, the Oropouche work). What we could not see, in the absence of your Research Statement and letters of recommendation, was evidence that you were the intellectual source of the scientific questions behind these projects rather than an excellent implementer of others' scientific vision. A Research Statement, which we did not have, would be the natural place for that evidence to live. Letters from Dr. Cannons at FDOH and from Dr. Grubaugh at Yale would further clarify this distinction. The committee expects your full dossier may support a higher score on this dimension.

## Resilience / Grit / Work Ethic — 3.0

This category produced our liveliest deliberation. Your statement shows sustained, high-stakes scientific work over more than four years, a promotion trajectory to supervisor of a seven-person department, a deliberately pursued academic collaboration with an external PI, and a well-reasoned career decision to pursue a PhD at a stage when staying put would have been easier. Three of our reviewers — Profs. Bemis and Chen, and G. Delgado — all read clear evidence of persistent behavior, initiative, and opportunity-seeking.

G. Delgado, our student reviewer, argued that the rubric's 4.0 Outstanding anchor language — "self-driven in finding resources and mentors," "actively seeking out opportunities" — is well-supported by specific evidence in your statement: cold-outreach to a Yale PI from a state health department position, methodology leadership on agnostic sequencing platforms, and being the first in the United States to obtain a full Oropouche genome. She scored 3.5. She also flagged that reviewers trained on traditional applicants (recent undergraduates with REU summers and independent projects) may under-count this kind of evidence because it reads as *professional* self-direction rather than *academic* self-direction, and she asked the committee to score on rubric merit rather than on pattern familiarity. The committee took this flag seriously.

Prof. Bemis agreed that the evidence is substantive but argued that the 4.0 Outstanding anchor typically requires corroboration through letters of recommendation, which are the primary evidence source for this category. In the absence of letters, honest scoring lands at 3.0. The committee resolved at 3.0 on this reasoning — not because your non-traditional initiative evidence was discounted, but because 3.5 or higher would require evidence we did not have. If your letters corroborate the pattern Delgado identified, your full-dossier score is likely higher than what we assigned.

The committee also noted, appreciatively, that your statement does not manufacture adversity. You describe the pandemic-era laboratory scale-up as collective institutional adaptation rather than as personal hero-narrative. That is a mature choice and it registered with us.

## Readiness / Fit for Program — 2.5 (category caps at 3.0)

The CMB rubric's key distinction at this level is between applicants who are **problem-driven** (3.0 Excellent — motivated by scientific questions, future research interests aligned with CMB faculty) and applicants who are **technique-motivated** (2.0 Good — grounded in biology and chemistry practice but not clearly problem-driven). Your statement sits between these anchors.

The final interests paragraph is problem-driven in a way we took seriously: developing and applying novel methods to track disease spread, understanding mechanisms that enable pathogens to jump between humans, vectors, and reservoirs, exploring vector biology for population control strategies in a changing climate. These are *questions*, framed at the right scale for a graduate research program, and they map cleanly onto CMB's virology, molecular and genome biology of microbes, and systems biology focus groups. Prof. Chen, whose lens is sharpest on fit, scored this criterion at 3.0 on the strength of the problem-driven framing combined with your demonstrable interdisciplinary scientific work.

G. Delgado scored 2.5 for a different reason: her student-side lens asks not just "can this applicant do the science" but "will this applicant thrive in CMB's specific student structure." Rotations are four weeks long and are designed to let you *start* a project rather than run one — a transition that may feel unfamiliar after supervising a department. She did not read this as a disqualifier but as a real conversation topic for an interview. Profs. Alder and Bemis scored 2.5 on a third consideration: transcripts are the primary evidence source for Readiness/Fit, and we did not have them. The reconciled 2.5 integrates all three lenses. If your transcripts show substantive coursework in biochemistry, organic chemistry, and advanced biology, this score is likely conservative.

## Why UW-Madison & CMB? — 2.5 (category caps at 3.0)

The rubric's 3.0 Excellent anchor asks for three things together: specific named CMB faculty and research areas, and mention of Madison atmosphere beyond the science. You named four CMB-affiliated faculty — Drs. Tom Friedrich, Kerri Coon, Dave O'Connor, and Adam Bailey. All four are well-chosen for your stated interests, and the committee read this as real homework, not name-dropping. That moved our score above the 2.0 Good anchor (general statements about the scientific environment).

What kept us below 3.0: the statement references the UW-Madison microbiology department's multi-disciplinary, collaborative approach (general, and not quite the CMB program by name), and does not engage with CMB-specific program structure (focus groups, rotations, student community) or with Madison as a place beyond its science. All four reviewers arrived at 2.5 independently and converged without disagreement on this criterion.

The important caveat: the CMB application invites a separate 400-word supplemental statement specifically about your interest in CMB, UW-Madison, and living in Madison. This committee did not see that statement. Many applicants put their strongest program-specific motivation content there. Our 2.5 reflects conservative scoring on the personal statement alone; we did not treat the absence of that content as evidence that the motivation does not exist.

## How the committee integrated these perspectives

All four reviewers independently arrived at a recommendation to invite you for an interview. That was the clearest signal of our deliberation. Where reviewers held different views, we weighted by designated lens: Prof. Alder for rigor, Profs. Bemis and G. Delgado for trajectory and initiative evidence, Prof. Chen for fit and program-specific engagement, and G. Delgado for the student-experience dimension of the program.

G. Delgado's rubric-fidelity flag about professional vs. academic self-direction shaped the Resilience deliberation explicitly. The committee did not discount her 3.5; the reconciled 3.0 reflects the evidence-limitation reality (no letters to corroborate) rather than a judgment that your professional initiative "doesn't count." It does count, and it was credited. The committee's honest position is that your full dossier — the Research Statement especially, but also letters and transcripts — may well tell a meaningfully stronger story than what the Personal Statement alone could convey. We are inviting you for an interview partly on the strength of that expectation.

We want to be transparent about one piece of uncertainty: the absence of your Research Statement is a significant evidence gap beyond the standard limitations of our simulated review. For an applicant in your situation — a career-changer with substantive professional scientific experience — the Research Statement is the document where the committee would most expect to see hypothesis-driven scientific thinking articulated in your own voice. We read the Personal Statement with that document missing, and our scores reflect that limitation.

## What this decision means

An invitation to interview is a positive outcome and a meaningful step. If you are invited to our interview weekend, you will have the opportunity to meet with CMB faculty whose research aligns with your interests (including, potentially, some of the faculty you named), to meet current CMB students and ask them directly about the program, and to explore focus groups in depth. The interview will also allow the committee to assess some of the things we could not see from the Personal Statement alone — including the self-direction question that shaped our Resilience deliberation and the student-experience fit question G. Delgado raised.

## For a future application — or future revisions

If you are invited to interview and want to think about how to present your scientific self most effectively, we offer these observations anchored to the rubric rather than to our preferences.

A stronger application on Engagement in Research, at the rubric's Outstanding (4.0) level, would show evidence that you yourself drove the intellectual direction of at least one project — that you formulated a scientific question, designed an experiment or analysis to address it, interpreted an ambiguous result, or identified a limitation that changed your own thinking. Your Personal Statement describes many excellent projects in "we" language; the rubric's Outstanding tier asks for at least one in clear "I asked, I designed, I interpreted" language. The Research Statement would be the natural home for this content. The Oropouche passage is close to this already — it could be even stronger if the reader could see the specific scientific question you were asking and how your interpretation of the result was yours.

A stronger application on Why UW-Madison & CMB? would add two dimensions to what you already have. First, specific engagement with CMB as a program (the 13 focus groups, the three four-week rotations, the cross-departmental structure that lets students draw on 200+ faculty) — not just the named faculty, but the program architecture that makes those faculty accessible to a rotating student. Second, some authentic engagement with Madison as a place to live and work for five or six years. These are fair questions the rubric asks; your supplemental statement likely addresses them already.
